Sorting Algorithms

Sorting is the most-studied algorithm for a reason — it teaches the whole toolkit. The headline: comparison sorts bottom out at O(n log n), and the differences are in the constants and trade-offs.

#dsa #sorting #algorithms #complexity

The idea in plain English

There's a proven lower bound: any sort that works by comparing elements needs at least O(n log n) comparisons in the worst case. So the practical choices among O(n log n) sorts come down to speed in practice, memory, and stability — not asymptotic class.

Quicksort partitions around a pivot and recurses. It's usually the fastest in practice thanks to excellent cache behavior and in-place work (O(log n) stack), but a bad pivot gives O(n²) worst case — mitigated by randomized or median-of-three pivots. Not stable.

Mergesort splits in half, sorts each, and merges. It guarantees O(n log n) even in the worst case and is stable, but needs O(n) extra space for the merge — the go-to when worst-case guarantees or stability matter (and for external/linked sorting). Heapsort is O(n log n) and in-place (O(1) space) but cache-unfriendly and not stable.

Below the comparison bound live counting and radix sort: O(n + k) by exploiting bounded integer keys (no comparisons). And the simple O(n²) sorts (insertion, bubble) aren't useless — insertion sort is fast on small or nearly-sorted inputs, which is why real library sorts (introsort, Timsort) switch to it for small partitions. Stability — preserving equal elements' order — matters when sorting by successive keys.

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

Standard libraries are pragmatic hybrids: .NET's Array.Sort uses introsort (quicksort that falls back to heapsort to dodge the O(n²) worst case, and insertion sort for tiny partitions), while Python and Java's object sort use Timsort (a stable mergesort/insertion hybrid tuned for real-world partially-ordered data). You rarely implement a sort, but you choose OrderBy (stable) vs Array.Sort (faster, unstable) based on whether you need stability — so knowing the trade-offs is practical, not academic.

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Comparison sorts can't beat O(n log n); choose among them by speed, space, and stability — not Big-O class.
  • Quicksort: fastest in practice, in-place, O(n²) worst; mergesort: stable + guaranteed but O(n) space; heapsort: in-place, not stable.
  • Counting/radix beat the bound (O(n+k)) for bounded integer keys; libraries use hybrids (introsort/Timsort) and switch to insertion sort for small inputs.
