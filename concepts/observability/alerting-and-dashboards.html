Alerting & Dashboards

Telemetry is useless if nobody looks until it's too late. Good alerting wakes a human only for real, actionable, user-facing problems; good dashboards show health at a glance.

#observability #alerting #dashboards #on-call

The idea in plain English

The cardinal rule of alerting: alert on symptoms, not causes. Page when users are affected — error rate up, latency past the SLO, the SLO's error budget burning fast — not on every internal cause like high CPU (which may be harmless). Cause-based alerts are noisy and miss novel failures; symptom-based alerts catch what actually matters.

Every page must be actionable and urgent. If an alert fires and the on-call's response is 'nothing to do' or 'I'll look tomorrow', it shouldn't have paged. The deadliest failure mode is alert fatigue: too many noisy alerts train people to ignore them, so the one real alert gets missed. Ruthlessly tune or delete noisy alerts.

Tier your responses: page (wake someone) only for urgent user impact; use tickets/notifications for things that need attention but not at 3 AM. Alert on the SLO burn rate so you page when the budget is being consumed dangerously fast, not on every transient blip.

Dashboards serve the other half: a top-level view (the golden signals — latency, traffic, errors, saturation) that answers 'is this service healthy?' in seconds, with drill-downs for investigation. The best dashboard is the one your on-call opens first during an incident and immediately knows where to look.

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

SRE-influenced on-call rotations page on a small number of symptom-based, SLO-burn alerts and route everything else to dashboards and tickets. Teams that ignored this learn the hard way: a wall of CPU/disk/queue alerts trains everyone to mute the channel, and then a real outage's alert is lost in the noise. The fix is always fewer, sharper, user-impact alerts plus a clean 'golden signals' dashboard per service.

Page on symptoms, dashboard the rest

In C#

YAML 
# Alert on a USER SYMPTOM + budget burn, not on a raw cause.
- alert: HighErrorRateBurn
  expr: error_budget_burn_rate{service="orders"} > 14   # fast burn
  for: 5m
  labels:    { severity: page }            # wake someone — actionable
  annotations: { summary: "orders burning error budget fast" }

# ❌ Avoid paging on causes that may not affect users:
#   alert on CPU > 80%  → noisy, trains people to ignore alerts.
# Dashboards: chart the golden signals (latency, traffic, errors, saturation).

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Alert on symptoms (user impact, SLO burn), not causes (CPU) — symptom alerts catch what matters and novel failures.
  • Every page must be actionable and urgent; alert fatigue from noisy alerts is the real danger — tune ruthlessly.
  • Dashboards show health at a glance via the golden signals, with drill-downs for investigation.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

Lead with 'alert on symptoms, not causes' and 'every page must be actionable,' then name alert fatigue as the failure mode to avoid. Tying alerts to SLO burn rate (not raw thresholds) shows modern, SRE-aware thinking.
