Two City Scheduling

Everyone could go to whichever city is cheaper for them, but the n-each constraint forces trade-offs — rank by how much you save by sending someone to A over B.

#greedy #sorting #medium

The problem

Given costs [costA, costB] for 2n people, send exactly n to each city to minimize total cost.

📥 Example

[[10,20],[30,200],[400,50],[30,20]] → 110

Brute force

Trying all ways to split into two equal halves is combinatorial. Sorting by the cost difference makes the greedy choice clear.

Optimized approach

Sort people by costA − costB ascending. The first n (most negative difference — biggest savings going to A) are sent to city A; the remaining n go to city B. Sum their respective costs.

Time O(n log n), Space O(1).

C# solution

C# 
public int TwoCitySchedCost(int[][] costs)
{
    Array.Sort(costs, (a, b) => (a[0] - a[1]) - (b[0] - b[1]));
    int n = costs.Length / 2, total = 0;
    for (int i = 0; i < costs.Length; i++)
        total += (i < n) ? costs[i][0] : costs[i][1];
    return total;
}

Complexity

⏱️ Complexity

  • Time: O(n log n) — dominated by the sort.
  • Space: O(1) — in-place sort.

Pattern & takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Sort by the savings A − B; the cheapest-for-A half goes to A.
  • The equal-split constraint is what makes naive per-person choice wrong.
  • An exchange argument proves the sorted assignment is optimal.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

The reframing — sort by how much each person saves by choosing A over B — is the crux. Send the top-saving half to A, the rest to B, and justify with an exchange argument.
