Responding to a Production Outage

An outage is graded on process, not heroics. Stop the bleeding first, communicate clearly, and only then hunt the root cause — with a blameless follow-up so it can't recur.

#incident-response #reliability #on-call #observability

❓ The interview question

“Production is down. Walk me through exactly what you do.”

How to think about it

Assess and declare. Confirm it's real and scope the severity — everything down, or one feature? How many users? Declare an incident with a clear severity level so the right people and urgency kick in; don't quietly debug a SEV-1 alone.

Communicate and assign roles. Open an incident channel, post to the status page, and name an incident commander (coordinates, decides) plus comms and ops roles. Clear ownership prevents the chaos of five people poking at the same thing.

Mitigate before you root-cause. The goal is restoring service, not understanding it yet. Reach for the fast levers: roll back the recent deploy (the usual culprit), fail over to a healthy region/replica, scale out, or flip a feature flag off. A known mitigation beats a perfect diagnosis the users are waiting on.

Diagnose with telemetry. Once stable (or in parallel), ask what changed (deploy, config, traffic, dependency) and drill metrics → traces → logs to localize tier → span → cause. The change log is your fastest lead.

Resolve and learn. Apply the durable fix, confirm recovery, then run a blameless post-mortem: timeline, root cause, and concrete action items (add the missing alert, the rollback automation, the test) so the same outage can't happen twice. Blameless because you want honesty, not scapegoats.

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

This mirrors Google SRE / PagerDuty incident-management practice: severity levels, an incident commander, a dedicated channel and status page, mitigate-then-diagnose, and a blameless post-mortem with tracked action items. The single most common root cause is a recent deploy, which is why a fast, rehearsed rollback is the most valuable button in the building — and why mature teams invest in feature flags and one-click rollback so 'mitigate first' is actually possible.

The incident flow

Make 'mitigate first' possible

config — rollback + flags + health gates 
# Fast rollback is the most valuable lever in an outage — automate it.
deploy:
  strategy: rolling
  autoRollback:
    onHealthCheckFailure: true        # bad deploy reverts itself
    onErrorRateAbove: 2%

# Feature flags let you disable a broken path without a deploy:
flags:
  new-checkout-flow: off              # flip off to mitigate instantly

# Readiness gates keep traffic off unhealthy instances during failover.

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Declare an incident and assign an incident commander — don't debug a major outage silently or alone.
  • Mitigate before root-causing: rollback, failover, scale, or feature-flag off; restoring service beats understanding it.
  • Diagnose via what-changed + metrics→traces→logs, then run a blameless post-mortem with concrete action items.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

The structure is the answer: assess → communicate (incident commander) → mitigate before diagnosing → fix → blameless post-mortem. Saying 'roll back first, understand later' and 'blameless' signals you've actually been on call, not just read about it.
