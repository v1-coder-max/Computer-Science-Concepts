Mitigating a DDoS Attack

You can't out-buy a botnet's bandwidth from your origin — you absorb at the edge and filter early, so the flood breaks against infrastructure far bigger than yours.

#security #ddos #scaling #incident-response

❓ The interview question

“Your site is being hit by a DDoS attack. How do you stay up?”

How to think about it

Classify the attack, because the defense differs. Volumetric floods your bandwidth (UDP/amplification); protocol attacks exhaust connection state (SYN floods); application-layer (L7) sends expensive-looking-but-valid requests (a flood of searches/logins) that are hardest to tell from real users.

Absorb volume at the edge. The first line is a CDN / DDoS-scrubbing service (Cloudflare, AWS Shield, Akamai) sitting in front via anycast: their global network soaks up terabits and only forwards clean traffic. You physically cannot absorb a large volumetric flood at a single origin — push it upstream.

Filter with a WAF and rate limits. A web application firewall blocks known-bad patterns and signatures; per-IP/per-key rate limiting caps abusers; IP-reputation and geo-rules drop obvious junk. For suspicious clients, a challenge (CAPTCHA / JS / proof-of-work) separates bots from humans cheaply.

Reduce origin cost and scale the rest. Cache aggressively so even traffic that gets through hits cache, not your database; autoscale to ride out the surge; and use a queue/load-shedding so the backend degrades gracefully instead of toppling.

Treat it as an incident: detect via traffic anomaly alerts, turn on the heightened protections (many are a config flip), communicate, and afterward review what got through. The honest point: pure-volumetric defense ultimately depends on your upstream provider's capacity, so having that in place before the attack is the real preparation.

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

Sites under attack route traffic through Cloudflare/Shield, flip on 'under attack' mode (JS challenges), tighten WAF rules and rate limits, and lean on cached responses so the origin barely notices. Record-breaking volumetric attacks (tens of millions of RPS) are absorbed by these providers' anycast networks, not by the target's servers. The teams that survive are the ones who had edge protection and caching configured beforehand; scrambling to add a CDN mid-attack is far harder.

Layers that blunt a flood

config — edge protection + caching + limits 
# 1. Front everything with a CDN / DDoS-scrubbing network (anycast).
#    Volumetric floods are absorbed there, not at your origin.

# 2. WAF rules + per-IP rate limit at the edge:
rate_limit:
  key: client_ip
  requests_per_minute: 600
  action: challenge          # JS/CAPTCHA challenge for suspicious clients

# 3. Cache aggressively so traffic that gets through hits cache, not the DB:
cache_control: "public, max-age=60, stale-while-revalidate=30"

# 4. Autoscale the origin + load-shed (429/503) beyond capacity.

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Classify the attack: volumetric (bandwidth), protocol (connection state), L7 (expensive valid-looking requests).
  • Absorb at the edge (CDN/scrubbing via anycast) — you can't soak a volumetric flood at a single origin.
  • Layer WAF + rate limits + challenges + caching + autoscale, and have it configured before the attack.
