CAP Theorem

When the network splits, a distributed datastore can keep answering or keep every answer correct — not both. CAP is the rule that forces the choice.

#cap #distributed-systems #consistency #availability

The idea in plain English

CAP names three properties a distributed system might want:

  • Consistency — every read sees the most recent write (all nodes agree).
  • Availability — every request gets a (non-error) response.
  • Partition tolerance — the system keeps working when the network between nodes drops messages.

The theorem: during a network partition you can guarantee at most two of the three. And here’s the practical bit — in any real distributed system, partitions will happen (networks fail), so P isn’t optional. That collapses the choice to: when a partition occurs, do you favor Consistency or Availability?

  • CP (consistency over availability) — on a partition, refuse requests it can’t answer correctly rather than return stale/conflicting data. Think bank balances. (e.g. traditional RDBMS clusters, MongoDB defaults, HBase, ZooKeeper.)
  • AP (availability over consistency) — keep serving on both sides of the split and reconcile later (eventual consistency). Think a social feed or shopping cart. (e.g. Cassandra, DynamoDB, Riak.)

The common myth is “pick any 2 of 3.” More accurately: P is mandatory, so it’s CP vs AP. And when there’s no partition, you still trade latency vs consistency — which is what the PACELC extension captures (“else, Latency or Consistency”).

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

The choice maps directly to “how bad is wrong data here?” A banking ledger picks CP: during a network split it would rather reject a transaction than risk a double spend. Amazon’s shopping cart famously picks AP (the Dynamo paper) — the cart must always accept adds even during a partition, and conflicting versions get merged later (you might briefly see a re-added item). Same company, opposite CAP decisions, because the cost of being temporarily inconsistent is wildly different.

How it shows up in config

Cassandra (AP, tunable consistency) 
-- Tunable per query: trade consistency for availability/latency.
CONSISTENCY ONE;     -- AP-leaning: any one replica answers (fast, may be stale)
CONSISTENCY QUORUM;  -- stronger: majority must agree (more consistent, less available)
-- If (reads + writes) consistency > replicas, you get strong consistency — at a cost.

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • Consistency, Availability, Partition tolerance — during a partition you can keep at most two.
  • Partitions are unavoidable, so the real choice is CP (correct, may refuse) vs AP (available, may be stale).
  • It’s decided per use case (bank = CP, cart/feed = AP); PACELC adds the latency-vs-consistency trade when there’s no partition.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

Don’t recite “pick 2 of 3.” Say P is mandatory, so it’s CP vs AP during a partition, and tie the choice to the use case (bank → CP, cart/feed → AP, eventual consistency). Mentioning PACELC (latency vs consistency without a partition) marks a strong answer.
