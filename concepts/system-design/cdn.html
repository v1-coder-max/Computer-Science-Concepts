CDNs

A Content Delivery Network puts copies of your content on servers near your users — so a page loads from down the street instead of across the planet.

#cdn #edge-caching #latency #origin-offload

The idea in plain English

Distance costs time: a request crossing oceans to your origin server adds hundreds of milliseconds. A CDN is a network of edge servers spread across the globe that cache your content close to users. A visitor is routed (via GeoDNS/anycast) to the nearest edge, which serves a cached copy — fast — and only contacts your origin on a cache miss.

You get three big wins:

  • Lower latency — content travels a short distance.
  • Origin offload — the edge absorbs most reads, so your servers and database do far less work.
  • Spike & attack resilience — the distributed edge soaks up traffic surges and absorbs DDoS.

CDNs are ideal for static assets (images, CSS, JS, fonts, video) and increasingly cache dynamic content too. Caching is governed by Cache-Control/TTL (see HTTP Caching); to ship a change you either wait for TTL, purge/invalidate the edge, or use versioned/hashed filenames. Pull CDNs fetch from origin on first request (lazy); push CDNs have you upload content ahead of time.

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

Netflix runs its own CDN (Open Connect): appliances inside ISPs cache popular shows so your stream comes from a box at your ISP, not a distant data center — essential for serving a huge share of the internet’s video. For the rest of the web, Cloudflare, Akamai, and CloudFront cache images, scripts, and pages at hundreds of edge locations, which is why a site feels equally fast in Tokyo and Toronto — and why the origin only sees a trickle of the actual traffic.

In practice

HTTP — what the origin tells the CDN 
HTTP/1.1 200 OK
Cache-Control: public, max-age=86400        # the edge may cache this for a day
Content-Type: image/webp

# Ship a new version by changing the URL (hashed filename) — no purge needed:
#   /assets/hero.4f9c2a.webp   →   /assets/hero.8b71de.webp

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • A CDN caches content on edge servers near users — lower latency, big origin offload, spike/DDoS resilience.
  • Routing is via GeoDNS/anycast to the nearest edge; the origin is hit only on a cache miss.
  • Control caching with Cache-Control/TTL and ship changes via purge or versioned filenames.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

When asked to make a site fast/global, put a CDN in front of static assets early. Explain edge caching, origin offload, and how you handle updates (TTL/purge vs hashed filenames). Mentioning GeoDNS routing and Netflix Open Connect shows real depth.
