How Netflix Streaming Works

Delivering smooth 4K video to hundreds of millions of devices on wildly varying networks comes down to two ideas: adaptive bitrate streaming and a CDN at the edge.

#streaming #adaptive-bitrate #cdn #case-study

The idea in plain English

You can’t ship one giant video file — networks vary by the second and devices differ. The solution is Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR), used by formats like HLS and DASH:

  • Encode once into many versions — each title is transcoded into multiple resolutions/bitrates (e.g. 240p … 4K).
  • Split into small chunks — each version is cut into a few-second segments, listed in a manifest file.
  • The client adapts — the player measures your bandwidth and, for each chunk, requests the highest quality it can sustain. Network dips → it drops to a lower bitrate mid-stream; recovers → it bumps back up. That’s why quality fluctuates but playback rarely stalls.

The chunks are served from a CDN edge close to you (Netflix runs its own, Open Connect, with appliances inside ISPs), and popular titles are pre-positioned at the edge before peak hours. Behind all this, a microservices control plane handles the parts that aren’t the video bytes — login, the browse/recommendations UI, playback authorization, DRM, and telemetry — so the heavy lifting (serving video) is decoupled from the smart bits (deciding what to play).

Real-world example

🏢 How this works in production

Netflix at peak is a double-digit percentage of global internet traffic, which is only possible because the video doesn’t come from Netflix’s data centers — it comes from Open Connect boxes inside your ISP. Their cloud (famously on AWS) runs the microservices control plane: recommendations, account, playback tokens, and the encoding pipeline that turns each new title into dozens of bitrate/resolution variants. When your show momentarily blurs then sharpens, you’re watching ABR pick a lower-then-higher chunk in real time.

The manifest the player reads

HLS manifest (conceptual) 
#EXTM3U
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=400000,RESOLUTION=320x180     # the player chooses a variant
low/index.m3u8                                            # ...by measured bandwidth
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=2000000,RESOLUTION=1280x720
mid/index.m3u8
#EXT-X-STREAM-INF:BANDWIDTH=8000000,RESOLUTION=3840x2160
high/index.m3u8
# each variant playlist lists ~4s .ts/.mp4 segments served from the nearest CDN edge

Key takeaways

🎯 Remember these three

  • ABR encodes each title into many bitrates, chunked into small segments; the player adapts per chunk to current bandwidth.
  • Chunks are served from CDN edges (Netflix’s Open Connect, inside ISPs), with popular content pre-positioned.
  • A microservices control plane handles browse/recommendations/auth/DRM — decoupled from serving the video bytes.

Interview tip

🗣️ When this comes up

“Design a video streaming service” → lead with ABR (multi-bitrate, chunked, client adapts) and a CDN for delivery (why the origin can’t serve the bytes). Mention the transcoding pipeline and that the control plane (recommendations/auth) is separate from streaming — that separation is the architectural insight.
